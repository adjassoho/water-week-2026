**SECTION I**
**BACKGROUND AND RATIONALE**
In a world increasingly shaped by climate change, demographic pressure, and challenges around food and water security, the water, sanitation and agriculture sectors are at the heart of sustainable development priorities. In West Africa and particularly in Benin, these sectors represent strategic levers for economic growth, public health, and community resilience. Yet their modernisation demands innovative, inclusive, and knowledge-driven approaches.
Digital innovation and inclusion have emerged as major catalysts for this structural transformation. Digitalisation of services, big data analytics, artificial intelligence, collaborative platforms and smart technologies offer unprecedented opportunities to improve governance, strengthen operational efficiency and promote evidence-based decision-making.
The 3<sup>rd</sup> Water and Sanitation Week brings together international experts, policy-makers, business leaders, field practitioners and local community representatives to create a dynamic ecosystem where research, innovation and technology transfer are continuously informed by real-world needs and emerging challenges.

**SESSIONS AND THEMATIC AREAS**
_The event is structured around four interconnected thematic areas, each encompassing scientific communications, keynote addresses and high-level panel debates. All areas welcome contributions from researchers, practitioners and students._
<div class="joplin-table-wrapper"><table><tbody><tr><th><p><strong>1</strong></p></th><th><p><strong>Session 1: Digital Transformation and Management of Water, Sanitation and Agriculture</strong></p><ul><li>Digital tools and integrated water resource management under flood and drought risks</li><li>Digital transformation and precision agriculture for sustainable water use</li><li>Digital innovation for sustainable urban and peri-urban water and sanitation governance</li></ul></th></tr></tbody></table></div>
**1.1. Digital Tools and Integrated Water Resource Management**
Floods and droughts represent major hydroclimatic risks in vulnerable urban, peri-urban and rural areas. Digital tools such as real-time hydrometeorological platforms, early warning systems, remote sensing, hydrological and hydraulic models, and climate databases support a better understanding of watershed dynamics and evidence-based decision-making. Digital inclusion strategies ensure that the knowledge produced is accessible to local communities and technical services through mobile technologies and citizen-science platforms.
**1.2. Precision Agriculture and Sustainable Agricultural Water Management**
Precision agriculture relies on soil moisture sensors, connected weather stations, satellite imagery, GIS and agro-hydrological models to optimise irrigation at the plot scale. In developing countries, the central challenge is extending digital access to smallholder farmers through simple tools (mobile apps, agricultural SMS) and adapted training. Research institutions play a pivotal role in contextualising these technologies for local realities and co-designing solutions with farmers.
**1.3. Digital Innovation for Urban and Peri-Urban Water and Sanitation**
Urban water and sanitation management faces rapid demographic growth, unplanned urbanisation, water pollution and infrastructure deficits. Digital technologies enable real-time network monitoring, leak detection, smart treatment plant management and sanitary risk mapping. In the sanitation domain, digital tools facilitate faecal sludge management and environmental impact assessment. Citizen participation and data transparency are essential to service quality and long-term system sustainability.

**2.1. Open Data and Water Governance**
Traditional water governance models are showing their limits in the face of growing pressures such as climate variability, agricultural intensification, and competing uses. Open data represents a strategic lever for transforming institutional frameworks and operational governance modes. Effective data openness requires standardisation, rigorous metadata structuring, interoperability of digital infrastructures and cross-sector integration, essential conditions for avoiding information silos and ensuring the comparability of results across territorial scales.
**2.2. Artificial Intelligence and Big Data for Water Risk Management**
Machine learning, predictive modelling and advanced time-series analysis enable exploitation of large heterogeneous datasets (hydrological, climatic, satellite-based and IoT sensor-generated) to improve early detection, forecasting and scenario simulation of water risks. These approaches support early warning systems, irrigation optimisation and decision-support tools for managers and public officials. Algorithmic robustness, transparency and associated ethical challenges are central concerns.
**2.3. Participatory Science and Co-Production of Knowledge**
Sustainable water management requires mobilising both scientific knowledge and local community expertise. Participatory science and collaborative data collection provide a privileged framework for co-producing knowledge and stimulating water innovation. By involving citizens, farmers and local stakeholders in monitoring water quality and quantity, these initiatives broaden the evidence base while reinforcing scientific legitimacy and institutional accountability. Digital participatory platforms integrate local knowledge into scientific tools and generate large-scale, territorially relevant data.

**3.1. Intelligent Adaptive Learning Systems and Academic Performance**
AI technologies open new perspectives for transforming pedagogical practices and improving learner performance. Adaptive learning systems design educational environments that dynamically adjust to individual needs, learning paces and cognitive profiles. By offering personalised learning pathways, immediate feedback and targeted remediation, these tools contribute to improving outcomes and reducing performance gaps, challenges particularly acute in African educational systems characterised by high student-to-teacher ratios. Such tools can strategically support pedagogical effectiveness without replacing the central role of the teacher.
**3.2. Strategic Use of Educational Data for Planning and Decision-Making**
In a context of growing enrolment, resource constraints and persistent access inequalities, the strategic exploitation of educational data constitutes a key lever for improving governance of educational systems. AI tools can analyse large volumes of educational statistics, academic performance data and socioeconomic indicators to identify trends, evaluate programme effectiveness and anticipate future infrastructure or staffing needs, providing decision-makers with concrete, evidence-based inputs for targeted policy actions.
**3.3. Sustainable and Equitable Digital Educational Ecosystems**
Integrating digital and AI technologies into African educational systems requires a systemic approach encompassing technological infrastructure, regulatory frameworks and digital competency development. Particular attention must be given to locally adapted solutions: low-bandwidth applications, offline functionality, and accessibility on widely available mobile devices. Contextualised innovation is the strategic lever for ensuring effective adoption and impact of digital tools including those incorporating AI features.

**4.1. Smart Faecal Sludge Treatment and By-Product Valorisation**
Modern sanitation strategies increasingly adopt a value-chain logic and inclusive business models. Integrating smart technologies such as modelling, sensors, automation and AI into faecal sludge treatment improves process efficiency, enables better flow control, and creates economic opportunities from sanitation by-products. These approaches offer lower operating costs and are particularly relevant for African cities where decentralised on-site sanitation systems remain predominant.
**4.2. Innovative Decentralised Wastewater Management**
Source-separation approaches in domestic wastewater management allow appropriate treatment of effluents based on their characteristics, and open pathways for the reuse of water, energy and fertilisers. Separately collected blackwater, containing the vast majority of nutrients and pathogens, requires specific treatment pathways. Modular integrated systems show strong potential for high-density urban areas without requiring centralised infrastructure. Recent advances in membrane technology are of particular relevance to this sub-theme.
**4.3. Digital Modelling of Membrane Performance for Drinking Water Treatment**
In a context of growing drinking water access challenges across Africa, membrane treatment technologies offer promising solutions for water quality improvement. Digital tools for predicting membrane structural performance, namely simulating particle and ion behaviour within filtration membranes and accounting for physicochemical interactions, contribute to optimising system design and operation. AI integration refines predictive models of solute dynamics in membrane active layers and opens perspectives for automated monitoring and process control.
**4.4. Machine Learning for Leak Detection and Drinking Water Network Management**
Drinking water supply networks, often developed incrementally and with limited planning, are characterised by great structural complexity, with thousands of interconnected nodes, pipes and equipment. Machine learning approaches, coupled with hydraulic modelling and simulation, enable the analysis of sensor data (flow, pressure, consumption), identification of anomalies, prediction of failures and optimisation of network zoning. These tools reduce losses, optimise technical interventions and improve service quality.

**SPECIAL SESSION 1**
**CROSS-PERSPECTIVES DEBATE**
_"Artificial Intelligence vs. Classical Approaches to Predicting Natural Phenomena: Complementarity or Opposition?"_
This special panel will illuminate the evolution of forecasting methods used in hydrology, agriculture and environmental science. Speakers will compare the performance, limitations and application domains of classical approaches (physical, statistical and empirical models) with AI-based methods drawing on machine learning and big data, in the prediction of floods, droughts, extreme rainfall, crop yields and other natural phenomena.
Particular attention will be given to data reliability, model interpretability, the role of human expertise, and the ethical and scientific challenges associated with the growing use of AI. The session will provide a dialogue space between experienced researchers and young digital specialists to explore complementarity between traditional knowledge, classical models and intelligent technologies in sustainable natural resource management.
<div class="joplin-table-wrapper"><table><tbody><tr><th><p><strong>Key debate axes:</strong></p><ul><li>Performance and limits of physical, statistical and empirical modelling approaches</li><li>Capabilities and constraints of machine learning and deep learning for environmental forecasting</li><li>Data reliability, model interpretability and the role of domain expertise</li><li>Convergence opportunities between traditional knowledge and intelligent technologies</li><li>Ethical and scientific challenges of growing AI use in natural resource management</li></ul></th></tr></tbody></table></div>
<div class="joplin-table-wrapper"><table><tbody><tr><th><p><strong>INDICATIVE PARTICIPANTS</strong></p><ul><li>Senior hydrologists and hydrometeorologists with expertise in classical modelling (HBV, SWAT, MIKE, GR4J)</li><li>AI and machine learning researchers applying deep learning and neural networks to environmental forecasting</li><li>Statisticians and climate scientists specialised in extreme event analysis and stochastic modelling</li><li>Representatives of national meteorological services and disaster risk reduction agencies (AGRHYMET, ACMAD, WASCAL)</li><li>Young researchers and PhD students working at the interface of AI and environmental sciences</li><li>Science journalists and communicators covering AI, climate and water policy topics</li></ul></th></tr></tbody></table></div>
**SPECIAL SESSION 2**
**UNESCO-IHE: WATER AND DEVELOPMENT PARTNERSHIP PROGRAMME (WDPP)**
_Water Diplomacy, Transboundary Governance and Capacity Building in West Africa_
**About the Programme**
Launched in 2008, the Water and Development Partnership Programme (WDPP) has been nurturing a community of scientists, practitioners and policymakers from over 40 countries, with the support of the Dutch Ministry of Foreign Affairs. Currently, IHE Delft coordinates two phases, phase 2 and 3, which run partly in parallel. While each phase has its own specific focus and targets, both aim to contribute to a peaceful, socially just and ecologically sustainable world ([read more](https://www.un-ihe.org/what-we-do/water-and-development-partnership-programme)).
**Session Objectives**
- Present the WDPP and its opportunities for professionals;
- Share experiences and results of projects funded and supported by the WDPP in West Africa;
- Discuss water governance, equity and ecological sustainability in the context of climate change;
- Explore synergies between West African universities and IHE Delft for joint training and collaborative research;
- Identify funding pathways for continuing WDPP initiatives in Africa.
